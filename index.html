The Process

From bag of flour to first bite

Pizza-making is a sequence of decisions, not a recipe to memorize. Each phase below has a purpose: hydrate the flour, build the gluten, develop flavor, shape without deflating, and bake fast enough to keep the air trapped inside. Get the philosophy right and the recipe writes itself.

Watch the dough, not the clock. Every kitchen is different — temperature, humidity, flour batch, even altitude. Numbers in recipes are starting points, not destinations. A principle every pizzaiolo learns the hard way

Phase 1 · Mixing & Hydration

Weigh everything in grams

Volume measurements are unreliable. A cup of flour can vary by 30g depending on how it's scooped. A digital kitchen scale is the single most important tool for consistent pizza. Aim for ±1g precision on flour, water, and salt; ±0.1g on yeast under 5 grams (a small jewelry-style scale handles this).

Combine flour and yeast first

Whisk instant dry yeast directly into the flour. Active dry yeast (ADY) is different: it needs to bloom in warm water (95–105°F) for 5–10 minutes first. Never let salt touch dry yeast directly for more than a couple of minutes — salt draws moisture out of yeast cells and weakens them.

Add water at the right temperature

Use the Water Temperature tool in the Calculator tab. Target a final dough temp of 75–80°F. Cooler water for hot kitchens or stand-mixer kneading (which generates significant friction heat); warmer water for cold kitchens or hand-mixing. Add water gradually — pour about 90%, mix, then add the remainder once the dough comes together. This prevents lumps and ensures even hydration.

Salt goes in last (or near-last)

Add salt after the flour has absorbed most of the water. Salt strengthens the gluten network and slows yeast activity — adding it last gives the yeast a head start and lets the gluten form more freely in the first few minutes.

Mix until just shaggy, then rest

Don't try to fully knead immediately. Mix only until no dry flour remains, then cover and rest for 20–40 minutes. This is the autolyse step — flour fully hydrates, enzymes (amylase, protease) start working, and gluten begins forming on its own. The dough that comes out of an autolyse needs about half the kneading time of an unrested dough, and it stretches better.

The autolyse trick is the single cheapest dough upgrade. No new equipment, no new ingredient — just a 30-minute pause after mixing. Pros use it; most home recipes skip it.

Phase 2 · Kneading & Gluten Development

Hand-knead 8–10 minutes

The "push, fold, turn" method: heel of your palm pushes the dough away, fold it back toward you, rotate 90°, repeat. The dough goes from rough and sticky to smooth and elastic. Resist adding flour — wet hands or a bench scraper handle stickiness better and preserve your hydration percentage.

Stand mixer: 5–7 minutes on low

Dough hook only. Low speed (KitchenAid speed 2) is plenty — high speed overheats and overworks the gluten. Stand mixers can over-knead in minutes; watch for the dough to clear the bowl walls and feel smooth, then stop. With high-hydration doughs above 70%, a mixer often outperforms hands.

No-knead method

For long cold ferments, you can skip kneading almost entirely. Mix until combined, then perform 3–4 "stretch and folds" at 30-minute intervals: grab one edge of the dough, stretch it up, fold it over the center, rotate the bowl, repeat. The slow fermentation builds gluten passively over 12–24 hours — like sourdough.

The windowpane test

Tear off a small piece. Stretch it gently between your fingers. If it forms a thin, translucent membrane you can almost see light through without tearing, gluten is fully developed. If it rips quickly, knead another 2–3 minutes and re-test. This works for any method, hand or machine.

Phase 3 · Bulk Fermentation

This is where flavor lives. Bulk fermentation is the time between the end of kneading and dividing the dough into balls. Long, slow bulk = complex flavor. Short, fast bulk = bland, bready dough.

Room temperature: 1–4 hours

For same-day dough: bulk on the counter, covered, until visibly puffed and ~50% larger. At 75°F kitchen temp, this takes 2–3 hours for a typical Neapolitan formula. Don't wait for it to double — that's overproofed for pizza.

Cold ferment: 24–72 hours

For maximum flavor: bulk on the counter 1 hour, then refrigerate in a sealed container. The cold doesn't stop fermentation — it slows yeast to a crawl while enzymes and bacteria keep developing flavor compounds. 24 hours is the minimum to notice the difference. 48–72 hours produces the deep, almost-sourdough character of a pro NY slice.

Folding during bulk (optional)

For higher-hydration doughs (above 68%), a "fold" every 30 minutes during the first 1–2 hours of bulk strengthens the gluten without kneading. Wet your hand, grab a quarter of the dough, stretch it up and over the center. Rotate 90°, repeat 3 more times. The dough becomes noticeably tighter.

Phase 4 · Dividing & Balling

Weigh each ball

Use a scale. Neapolitan: 200–280g. New York / general: 250–310g. Detroit: 280–340g (depending on pan size). Sicilian: 600–800g (full pan). Within ±5g of target — unevenness leads to uneven baking.

Shape into tight, seamed balls

The Neapolitan technique called staglio resembles shaping mozzarella: gather the edges, pinch underneath while rotating, creating a smooth taut top and a pinched seam on the bottom. Other methods (Vito Iacopelli style) use oiled hands and a cutting-and-shaping motion against the counter. Whatever method you use, the surface should be smooth and tight — that surface tension is what supports the rise.

Rest in covered containers

Lightly oil a container, place ball seam-down, with at least 2x the ball's volume of headspace. Cover airtight — a dried-out skin is one of the worst things that can happen to dough, and it can't be reversed.

Second proof: 4–8 hours room temp, or finish cold ferment

After balling, balls need a second rest — the secondo apretto in Neapolitan tradition. For same-day pizza: 4–6 hours at room temperature. For cold-fermented dough: ball at the start of cold ferment, or ball partway through, depending on your timing.

Phase 5 · Stretching

This is where most home bakers struggle. The dough was perfect — and then they killed it with a rolling pin or kept stretching it after it started fighting back.

Come to room temperature first

Cold dough tightens up and tears. Pull dough from the fridge at least 1–2 hours before you plan to stretch. The dough should feel pillowy, jiggle when poked, and stretch when nudged — not snap back like a rubber band.

Flour generously, then more

Coat the dough ball on both sides with flour or semolina. Don't be precious about it — excess flour brushes off before launching. Semolina is the pro's secret: coarser than flour, it acts like ball-bearings under the pizza, preventing sticking without burning at high heat.

Press the center, not the rim

With the dough flat in front of you, place your fingertips just inside the edge and press down firmly, working outward in a spiral. Leave a 1-inch (2.5cm) border untouched — that's your cornicione. You're pushing gas into the edge, not out of it. The center should be thin; the edge should remain plump.

The knuckle stretch (Neapolitan)

Drape the dough over the backs of your loose-fisted knuckles. Let it hang. Gravity does most of the work. Rotate slowly, letting it stretch evenly. A 250g ball should stretch to a 10–12 inch disc. The dough almost stretches itself once gluten is properly developed.

Never use a rolling pin

A rolling pin compresses and deflates every air bubble you spent 24+ hours building. The result is a flat, dense, cracker-like crust. The only exception is intentionally cracker-thin styles like Roman tonda, where this is the goal.

Phase 6 · Saucing & Topping

Sauce first, sparingly

Use the back of a ladle in a spiral motion from center outward, leaving the cornicione naked. About 3–4 tablespoons for a 12-inch pizza. Too much sauce = soggy center.

Less is more with cheese

Especially for Neapolitan. Tear fresh mozzarella into thumb-sized pieces and scatter — don't cover. The dough needs to breathe between cheese spots. For NY-style with low-moisture mozzarella, a thin even layer covering the sauce is correct.

Wet toppings last

Tomatoes, fresh mozzarella, and anything releasing water should go on right before the oven, or even after baking (basil, prosciutto, arugula). Heavy wet toppings stewed on the dough for 5 minutes will glue it to the peel.

Phase 7 · Launching

Top on the peel, not the counter

Move the stretched dough to a floured (or semolina-dusted) peel before saucing. Topped pizza left on a counter develops moisture underneath and sticks like glue.

Shake-test before launching

Give the peel a small fore-and-aft shake. The pizza should slide freely. If it's stuck, lift the edge with a bench scraper and shoot more semolina underneath. A stuck launch is the #1 home-pizza disaster.

Launch with confidence

Tip the peel to about 20°, position the front edge where you want the pizza to land, push the peel slightly forward, then in one smooth motion pull the peel out from under. Hesitation is the enemy. The pizza should land flat where the front edge of the peel was.

Phase 8 · Baking

Heat is everything. Pizza is fundamentally a hot-floor problem: the bottom needs to crisp before the top burns. This is why pro ovens have a 700°F+ deck and why home ovens with a baking steel are the closest you can get.

The single biggest home upgrade: a 1/4-inch baking steel preheated for 60+ minutes at maximum oven temperature, top rack, then broiler on for 5 minutes before launch. Steel conducts heat about 18–20× faster than ceramic stone. This is what makes a 500°F home oven approximate a 900°F wood-fired floor.

Phase 9 · Eating

The Neapolitan is served immediately, soft enough to be eaten with a fork and knife or folded a portafoglio (wallet-style). The NY slice rests 60 seconds, then is held flat with the cheese-end folded lengthwise. Detroit gets cut into rectangles and served in the pan. The pizza you make is meant to be eaten while it's still steaming. Pictures are fine, but don't take too many.

The Pre-Ferment

Where pizza becomes great instead of good

A pre-ferment is a small portion of the final dough mixed 8–24 hours in advance. It ferments separately, then gets added back into the main mix. This single technique is the difference between supermarket pizza and the pizza you remember.

Why it works: the long pre-ferment lets enzymes break starch into sugar, bacteria produce organic acids that add flavor and digestibility, and the yeast develops a deep, almost wine-like complexity. None of this happens in a one-hour rise.

There are three main pre-ferment types — they're not interchangeable, but they all aim at the same goal.

Poolish

Origin: Polish (hence the name), refined by French bakers in the 19th century. Today the most popular pre-ferment in modern Neapolitan pizza.

Hydration: 100% — equal weights of flour and water, plus a pinch of yeast.

Famous champion: Vito Iacopelli, the third-generation Neapolitan pizzaiolo whose YouTube poolish tutorial has been viewed millions of times, made this technique synonymous with elite home pizza. His formula adds a small amount of honey to feed the yeast and produce a more active ferment.

Vito's classic poolish formula (for 6 dough balls):
• 300g flour · 300ml water · 3–5g instant dry yeast · 5g honey
Mix until smooth, rest 1 hour at room temp, then refrigerate 16–24 hours.
The next day, combine with 700g flour, 400ml water, 30g salt, and 10g olive oil for the final dough. Bulk 1 hour, ball, then cold ferment another 24 hours before use.

What it looks like ready: Triple in volume, visibly bubbly across the surface, smells sweet and yeasty with a faint alcohol note. Should fall back into itself when you tap the container.

Best for: Neapolitan, Roman al taglio, almost any pizza style. Especially good for home ovens — the poolish improves crust texture and oven spring.

Biga

Origin: Italian, especially northern Italy. Older, stiffer cousin of poolish.

Hydration: 50–60% — much stiffer than poolish, more like a regular bread dough.

What it tastes like: Cleaner and sharper than poolish. Biga produces a more open crumb structure and a slightly tangy, almost-sour flavor — closer to traditional Italian bread.

Typical formula: 500g flour · 250ml water · 2g IDY. Mix into a shaggy mass — don't knead — and refrigerate 16–24 hours.

Best for: Roman al taglio, Pinsa, hearty pan styles. Detroit and Sicilian benefit from biga's structural strength.

Levain (Natural Starter / Sourdough)

Origin: The ancient method. Predates commercial yeast by thousands of years. Pizza in Naples was originally leavened this way.

What it is: A self-sustaining culture of wild yeast and lactic bacteria, kept alive by daily flour and water feedings. No commercial yeast needed.

What it tastes like: Deepest, most complex flavor of any pre-ferment. Subtle sourness, profound aroma, exceptional digestibility — many people who struggle with commercial-yeast pizza find sourdough pizza much easier on the stomach.

The trade-off: Requires daily commitment to keep the starter alive. Fermentation timing is less predictable than commercial yeast. Once mastered, produces pizza that doesn't have a peer.

Direct Dough (No Pre-Ferment)

The straight dough method: all ingredients mixed at once, fermented in one stage. It's faster, simpler, and absolutely capable of making excellent pizza — especially if you cold-ferment for 24+ hours, which is itself a kind of slow pre-ferment.

Modern home techniques like Ken Forkish's "24 to 48-Hour Pizza Dough" demonstrate that long cold fermentation of a direct dough can rival pre-fermented results. The key word is long — anything under 24 hours of cold ferment will taste flat.

The Five Ingredients

Flour, water, salt, yeast — and what they're for

An authentic Neapolitan pizza by AVPN rules contains exactly four ingredients in the dough: flour, water, salt, yeast. Most American styles add a fifth, olive oil. That's it. Everything below is detail on each one.

Flour

The most important ingredient. Pizza flour quality determines everything: extensibility, oven spring, crust color, mouthfeel, even digestibility.

Italian "00" Flour

The Italian flour grading system (00, 0, 1, 2, integrale) describes grind fineness, not protein content. "00" (doppio zero) is the most finely milled — almost talcum-powder smooth. 00 alone tells you nothing about whether the flour is right for pizza. You must also know the protein content and the W rating.

The W rating (force/strength) is unique to Italian flours and measures how much air a dough bubble can hold before bursting in an alvéograph test. It ranges from ~120 (weak) to 400+ (very strong). Higher W = longer fermentation tolerance.

American flours

American flour packaging usually shows only protein content. There is no W rating because American mills don't measure it. As a rough rule:

  • All-purpose (10–11.5% protein): too weak for serious pizza, struggles past 12-hour ferment
  • Bread flour (12–13% protein): great workhorse, equivalent to a medium-W 00
  • High-gluten / "pizza" flour (13.5–14.5%): the NY-style standard, like King Arthur Sir Lancelot
  • Tipo 00 imported: works in home ovens but at 500°F you may want stronger flour for proper oven spring
Common myth: 00 flour is always best for pizza. Reality: 00 is only ideal at extreme temperatures (700°F+). At 500°F home-oven temps, you may get better oven spring from a stronger bread flour. Many top home pizzaiolos use a 50/50 blend of Caputo 00 and high-gluten bread flour.

Water

Water is 60–80% of a dough's weight. Its quality matters more than most people realize.

Hardness: Moderately hard water (60–120 mg/L calcium) is ideal. AVPN regulations specify 60–80 mg/L. Hard water strengthens gluten and yields predictable doughs. Very soft water (under 50 mg/L) makes slack, weak dough. Very hard water (above 200 mg/L) makes dough tough and slows fermentation.

Chlorine: Heavy chlorination kills yeast activity. If your tap water smells like a pool, let it sit uncovered for several hours so chlorine off-gases — or use filtered water. Chloramine, used by some municipalities, doesn't off-gas; filter it through activated carbon.

pH: Aim for 6–7 (slightly acidic to neutral). Most municipal water falls in this range. Strongly alkaline water (above 8) interferes with fermentation.

Salt

Salt has three jobs: flavor, gluten strengthening, and fermentation control. Below 1.5% (baker's percent), the dough tastes flat and gluten is weak. Above 3.5%, the dough tastes salty and yeast activity is suppressed.

Pizza-style salt ranges: Neapolitan 2.8–3.0% · NY 2.0–2.5% · Detroit 2.0–2.5% · Roman al taglio 2.5%.

Type: Sea salt (preferred by AVPN), kosher salt, fine non-iodized table salt — all fine. Iodized salt can impart a slight metallic taste at high concentration; avoid for pizza if possible. Coarse salt should be dissolved in the water first, or used in lower volume since it weighs more per teaspoon than fine.

Yeast

Pizza yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) comes in three forms. All three eat sugar, produce CO₂ and ethanol, and contribute flavor. They differ in concentration and how you use them.

TypeUse ratioHow to use
Instant (IDY)1× (baseline)Mix directly into flour
Active dry (ADY)1.25×Bloom in warm water 5–10 min first
Fresh / Cake3×Crumble into water or flour

If a recipe calls for 1g IDY, use 1.25g ADY or 3g fresh. Fresh yeast (highly perishable, refrigerated, popular in Italy and AVPN-preferred) gives slightly more aromatic flavor but is rare in American grocery stores.

How much yeast to use: Less than you think. Long ferments need very little yeast — 0.1% or less of flour weight for a 24-hour cold ferment. The Calculator's yeast hint suggests an amount based on your ferment time and temperature.

Olive Oil

Olive oil is excluded from traditional AVPN Neapolitan dough — it's added only at the end, drizzled on the finished pizza. American and Roman styles, however, incorporate oil at 1–3% for tenderness and browning aid.

What oil does in dough: Coats gluten strands, slightly weakening structure and producing a softer, more tender crumb. Helps the crust crisp at lower bake temperatures (essential for home ovens). Carries fat-soluble flavor compounds.

What to buy: Real extra-virgin olive oil from a single origin, harvest date on the label, in a dark glass bottle. The peppery throat-burn from a good Italian or Spanish EVOO comes from oleocanthal, an antioxidant — that bite is the marker of freshness.

What to avoid: Vague "imported from Italy" labels, plastic bottles, anything more than 18 months past harvest. Most American supermarket "extra virgin" is rancid by the time you buy it.

Tomatoes

For Neapolitan-style pizza, the gold standard is San Marzano DOP — a specific variety of plum tomato grown in volcanic soil south of Naples. Look for the yellow-and-red DOP seal and the phrase "Pomodoro S. Marzano dell'Agro Sarnese-Nocerino DOP." Brands like Gustarosso, Rega, and Strianese are reliable.

"San Marzano-style" on the label means nothing — it's a marketing term, not a designation. Most cans labeled San Marzano in American supermarkets aren't the real thing.

The best non-San-Marzano: Bianco DiNapoli (California, organic, processed quickly after harvest). Cento Certified San Marzano with the dual seal. For Detroit-style, Stanislaus 7/11 or "Tomato Magic" are pizzeria favorites.

Sauce technique: Pizza sauce is usually not cooked. Crush whole peeled tomatoes by hand or pulse briefly in a food processor — don't liquefy. Add a pinch of salt and a drizzle of olive oil. That's it. Cooking happens on the pizza.

The tomato sauce on the pizza is the key element and it must strictly be from a can. This is to guarantee consistency, quality, and great flavor. Raffaele Lotto, Executive Chef, Pizzeria Cucina, Venice

Cheese

Two main families: fresh (high moisture) and aged (low moisture). Each behaves completely differently on pizza.

Mozzarella di Bufala Campana DOP

Buffalo milk mozzarella from Campania. Tangier, richer, wetter than cow's milk. The AVPN-approved cheese for true Neapolitan. Tear by hand into pieces and drain on paper towels before topping — buffalo mozzarella is roughly 60% water and will flood your pizza if you don't.

Fior di Latte

"Flower of milk" — cow's milk fresh mozzarella. Milder, drier, more forgiving than buffalo. The most common cheese on Neapolitan pizza in Naples itself. Look for the Fior di Latte Appennino Meridionale DOP if you want the AVPN-spec cheese.

Low-Moisture Mozzarella

The American pizzeria standard. Aged longer than fresh mozzarella, then shredded or sliced. Melts evenly, browns nicely, won't soak the crust. The cheese for New York and Detroit pizza. Whole-milk versions taste richer than part-skim.

Brick Cheese

The defining cheese of Detroit-style. Originally a Wisconsin invention (Buddy's used it because it was locally cheap). Buttery, slightly sharper than mozzarella, exceptional browning behavior. If unavailable, a 50/50 blend of low-moisture mozzarella and mild white cheddar gets you close.

Provolone

Common on New York pizza (often blended with mozzarella). Sharper, more aromatic. Aged provolone (provolone piccante) is too strong for pizza; use young (dolce).

Parmigiano-Reggiano & Pecorino Romano

Used as finishing cheeses, grated on after baking. Not melted. Adds umami depth. The real ones (DOP, aged 24+ months) are worlds away from green-can imitations.

The Cornicione's Best Friends

Beyond the basics, these are the small upgrades that mark a serious pizzaiolo:

  • Sea salt finishing flake (Maldon, fleur de sel) — scattered on the cornicione post-bake
  • Fresh basil — added after baking, never before (it burns black in seconds at high temp)
  • Hot honey — drizzled on spicy pies (jalapeño, soppressata) for sweet/heat balance
  • Garlic confit — slow-cooked garlic in olive oil; brushed on cornicione before launching
  • Calabrian chiles — for spicy pies, vastly better than red pepper flakes

Troubleshooting

Tap any problem to diagnose · 14 common issues

Most likely Hydration too high for your flour. Strong flour (13%+ protein) handles 70%+ hydration; weaker flour struggles past 65%.

Also possible Under-kneaded — the gluten hasn't fully absorbed the water yet. Knead 3–5 more minutes and re-check.

Could be Diastatic malt in your flour. This converts starch to sugar and makes the dough surface tacky regardless of hydration.

Quick fix Wet your hands and use a bench scraper. Generous flour or semolina on the work surface. Resist dumping flour into the dough — it changes the formula.

Next time Drop hydration 2–3% or switch to a stronger flour (more protein, higher W rating).

First diagnose Tight and springy (snapping back) or soft and slack (spreading and tearing)? Tight = under-kneaded, under-rested, or too cold. Slack = overproofed or over-hydrated.

Tight & tearing Cover and rest 15–30 more minutes. Cold dough straight from the fridge always tears — wait 1–2 hours at room temperature minimum.

Slack & tearing Overproofed — the gluten network has broken down. There's no full recovery; reball gently if you can. Less yeast or shorter ferment next time.

Always tearing Insufficient gluten development. Try a 20–30 minute autolyse before kneading. Knead longer or use stronger flour.

Tears at the same spot A thin spot you created while stretching. Push gas back into that area gently with your fingertips, then continue stretching.

Most likely Yeast is dead or expired. Test: 1/4 tsp yeast + 1/4 cup 100°F water + pinch sugar. Should foam in 5–10 minutes. If nothing, throw the yeast out.

Water too hot Above 130°F kills yeast outright. Above 110°F slows it dramatically. Cool tap water (60–80°F) is safest if unsure.

Room too cold Below 65°F, yeast activity drops significantly. Try the oven-with-light-on trick: closed oven with the interior light makes a warm proof box (~80°F).

Salt killed it Salt poured directly onto dry yeast and left for 5+ minutes draws moisture from yeast cells and damages them. Always mix salt into flour first.

Cold-fermented expectation Cold-fermented dough barely rises in the fridge by design. The rise happens after you pull it out and bring it to room temp.

Why it happens Topped pizza sat on the peel too long. Moisture from sauce and dough seeped into the flour bed and turned it into paste.

Prevention Build the pizza on the peel and launch within 60–90 seconds. Have toppings prepped and ready before you stretch.

Better dust Semolina flour or rice flour rolls under the dough like ball-bearings. Regular flour absorbs water and glues.

Stuck right now Lift the stuck edge with a bench scraper or knife and shoot semolina under it. Shake the peel — if it slides, launch immediately.

Try a screen or parchment A perforated pizza screen or parchment paper underneath the pizza eliminates the sticking risk entirely (parchment chars but holds; screens stay in for the bake).

Most likely Oven not hot enough, or stone/steel not preheated long enough. Steels need a full 45–60 minutes at max temp.

Overproofed dough Long fermentation eats sugars. Fewer sugars = less Maillard browning. Crust stays pale even when fully baked through.

Quick fix Hit it with the broiler for 30–60 seconds at the end. Watch closely — pale to charred is about 45 seconds.

For NY/Detroit Adding 1–2% sugar (or honey, or malt) to the dough increases Maillard browning at lower bake temperatures. Authentic NY pies often include this.

Most likely Underbaked. Bottom can look done while center is still wet. Probe the cornicione: done is 195–210°F internal.

Too many toppings Wet sauce or piles of cheese steam the dough from above. Less is more — Neapolitan pizza in Naples is shockingly bare by American standards.

Cold dough launched too soon Cold dough resists internal cooking. Room temp at least 1–2 hours before stretching.

Underproofed Dough that didn't ferment long enough never developed the open structure needed to bake through cleanly. The texture stays dense and bread-like.

Gum line under sauce The classic underbake symptom. Thinner sauce, less moisture, more bake time, or hotter deck. Sometimes both — use a baking steel.

Most likely Underproofed — gluten is still tight and tense, hasn't relaxed.

Fix now Cover and rest 30 minutes to an hour. The gluten will relax and stretching becomes easy.

Cold from fridge Pull dough 1–2 hours before stretching. Cold gluten is rubber-band stiff; warm gluten flows.

Over-kneaded Especially with stand mixers. Over-kneaded dough fights stretching for hours.

Rack too high Top rack with broiler effect overcooks the top. Move steel to middle rack for more even bake.

Broiler too long If finishing with broiler, 30–60 seconds is plenty.

Need heat from below Baking steel (vs. stone) conducts heat 18× faster from below. The bottom gets char before the top burns.

Oven thermostat lying Many home ovens read 50°F low. Verify with an oven thermometer.

Oven too cool Common in Ooni-style outdoor ovens. The deck stays hot while the dome cools without flame. Re-fire the burner or shift the pizza partway through.

Steel too hot If using broiler-finish technique, the steel may be saturating at very high temperatures. Move to lower rack or skip pre-broil.

Pizza in one spot too long In Ooni and Roccbox ovens, rotate the pizza 90° every 20 seconds. The side closest to the flame cooks fastest.

Heat not high enough Leopard spotting requires 700°F+ deck temperature. Home ovens at 500°F with steel + broiler can produce light spotting, but not the dramatic char of a wood-fired oven.

Dough overproofed Gas already escaped before baking. The pizza lays flat and won't puff.

Dough underproofed Not enough trapped gas to puff in the first place. Longer ferment.

Rolling pin used Crushes every bubble built during fermentation. Hand-stretch only for serious oven spring.

Cheese too fatty Whole-milk low-moisture mozzarella has higher fat than part-skim. Some greasing is normal; pools mean too much cheese, too much fat, or both.

Cheese too wet Fresh mozzarella is 50%+ water. Drain it thoroughly on paper towels (15–30 minutes) before topping.

Use less 4–5 oz of cheese on a 12-inch pizza is plenty for most styles. Less is more.

Pre-shredded cheese Bagged shredded mozzarella is coated with anti-caking agents (starch, cellulose) that interfere with proper melt and can release as gritty residue. Always shred your own.

Tomatoes weren't drained Whole peeled tomatoes have a lot of juice. Strain through a sieve before crushing for thicker sauce.

Don't blend smooth Smoothie-texture sauce sits on the dough as liquid. Pulse or crush by hand so there's still pulp texture.

Too much sauce 3–4 tablespoons for a 12" Neapolitan. Less for thin styles.

Wet brand Some brands of canned tomatoes are extremely juicy. Try Bianco DiNapoli, Mutti, or genuine San Marzano DOP for better consistency.

Not enough oil Detroit pans need a generous 2–3 tablespoons of oil — the bottom should look like the dough is swimming. Pan-frying the bottom is the goal.

Wrong pan Anodized blue steel Detroit pans (LloydPans, Detroit Style Pizza Co.) release like nonstick. Cheap pans require more oil and patience.

Cheese stuck to pan The frico edge needs cheese pressed all the way to the corners — but cheese alone (without oil under it) glues to the pan. Always oil first.

Stuck right now Run a thin knife or offset spatula around the perimeter, then under one corner. Lift gently. If it really won't come, pop it back in the oven for 2 minutes to re-melt the cheese-pan bond.

Over-fermented Too long at too warm a temperature. Lactic and acetic acids have built up. The dough may still be salvageable but flavor will be off.

Too warm too long Bulk fermentation above 80°F for many hours produces excessive acid. Keep bulk ferments in 70–75°F range.

Slight tang is good A faint, pleasant tang from a long cold ferment is desirable — it's the sign of healthy lactic activity. A sharp, vinegary smell is not.

A 20–60 minute rest after mixing flour and water, before adding salt and yeast (or before kneading). The flour fully hydrates, enzymes begin converting starch to sugar, and gluten starts forming without any work from you. Result: less kneading needed, more extensible dough, often better flavor. Invented by French baker Raymond Calvel in 1974.

Associazione Verace Pizza Napoletana. Founded in Naples in 1984 by Antonio Pace and Lello Surace to codify and protect the rules of true Neapolitan pizza-making. Issues certifications to pizzerias worldwide that follow strict regulations on ingredients, dough, oven, and technique. The art of Neapolitan pizza-making was added to UNESCO's intangible cultural heritage list in 2017.

The standard system for expressing dough recipes. Flour is always 100%. Every other ingredient is a percentage of flour weight. Water at 65% means 650g water per 1000g flour. The math makes scaling trivial and lets you compare any recipe to any other regardless of batch size.

An Italian stiff pre-ferment: flour, water (around 50–60% hydration), and a tiny amount of yeast, mixed 16–24 hours before the main dough. Adds complex flavor and structural strength. Particularly suited to long-fermented Roman pizza al taglio and pinsa. Compare to poolish, the wetter French version.

The first rise — when dough ferments as one big mass before being divided into balls. Most flavor and structure development happens here. Length varies: 30 minutes to 24+ hours depending on temperature and yeast amount.

Letting dough rise slowly in the refrigerator, typically 24–72 hours. Cold slows yeast activity dramatically but doesn't stop enzymatic and bacterial action that develops flavor. Cold-fermented dough is more flavorful, more extensible, and more digestible than same-day dough.

(Cor-ni-CHO-nay.) The raised, puffy edge of a Neapolitan pizza. AVPN regulations specify a height of 1–2 cm, swollen and free from burns. The cornicione forms because the pizzaiolo pushes air outward from the center while stretching, never compressing the rim.

The temperature you want your dough to be at the end of mixing — typically 75–80°F for pizza. Hit it by adjusting your water temperature using: water = (DDT × 3) − room temp − flour temp − friction. Friction is heat added by mixing: ~5°F for hand kneading, 15–25°F for a stand mixer.

EU geographic protection designations. DOP (Protected Designation of Origin): ingredient must come from a specific region (San Marzano tomatoes, Mozzarella di Bufala Campana). IGP (Protected Geographic Indication): looser regional link. STG (Traditional Specialty Guaranteed): protects a method rather than an origin (pizza Napoletana itself is STG-protected).

"Flower of milk." Cow's milk fresh mozzarella, common on Neapolitan pizza in Naples itself. Drier and milder than buffalo mozzarella. The DOP version is Fior di Latte Appennino Meridionale.

The crispy, caramelized cheese edge that defines Detroit-style pizza. Cubed brick cheese is pushed all the way to the pan walls; in the hot oven, it melts, oozes against the metal, and crisps into a lacy golden crust around the perimeter.

The protein network formed when wheat flour meets water and gets agitated (kneading, mixing, folding). Made of two proteins — glutenin (strength) and gliadin (extensibility) — that bond into a stretchy web. Gluten traps the gas yeast produces; that's how dough rises and bread holds shape.

The doughy, undercooked stripe just below the sauce in an underbaked pizza. Caused by overly wet sauce, too many toppings, dough too cold at launch, or insufficient bottom heat. The pizza is overcooked on the bottom and uncooked in this layer.

Water as a percentage of flour weight. 60% = 600g water per 1000g flour. Higher hydration = more open crumb, lighter texture, but stickier and harder to handle. Lower hydration = easier to work with but denser.

IDY (Instant Dry Yeast) goes straight into the flour; no proofing needed. ADY (Active Dry) needs to bloom in warm water first; use ~25% more than IDY. Fresh (compressed cake yeast) is highly perishable; use about 3× the IDY weight. All do the same job: eat sugar, produce CO₂ and ethanol, contribute flavor compounds.

The dark, almost-black blistered char marks on a properly baked Neapolitan crust. Caused by extreme heat (800–900°F) puffing the dough's surface into bubbles that briefly touch the dome of a wood-fired oven. Marks of authenticity, not burning.

A self-sustaining culture of wild yeast and lactic bacteria, kept alive by daily flour and water feedings. Replaces commercial yeast. Produces the deepest, most complex pizza flavor and the most digestible dough — but requires ongoing care.

The browning chemistry between amino acids and reducing sugars that creates golden color and toasty flavor in baked crust (and seared meat). Different from caramelization (sugar browning alone). Needs heat above ~285°F (140°C). Discovered by Louis-Camille Maillard in 1912.

The Neapolitan technique of shaping dough balls. The motion mimics how fresh mozzarella is shaped (mozzare = to cut/pinch). A portion of dough is pinched off, then sealed with the thumb against the palm in a rotating motion to create a smooth-topped, tightly seamed ball.

Overproofed: dough has fermented too long. Gluten has broken down, structure is collapsing, dough is slack, gas has escaped, surface looks deflated. Bakes pale and dense.

Underproofed: dough hasn't fermented enough. Gluten is tight, snaps back when stretched. Bakes into a dense, gummy, bread-like crust without open crumb.

An oval-shaped Roman pizza made with a flour blend (wheat, soy, and/or rice flour), very long cold fermentation (48–72h), and high hydration (80%+). Lighter and crisper than traditional pizza, marketed as more digestible. Trademarked by Pinsa Romana SRL.

A wet (100% hydration) pre-ferment originating in Polish baking. Equal weights of flour and water, a tiny amount of yeast, sometimes a touch of honey. Mixed 12–18 hours before the main dough. The hallmark technique of modern Neapolitan pizza-making, popularized by Vito Iacopelli.

"Dough point" — the moment in mixing when a Neapolitan pizzaiolo declares the dough complete. Signaled by elasticity, smooth surface, and the dough cleanly leaving the mixer bowl walls. AVPN regulations specify ~20 minutes total mixing time at low speed.

(Skro-kee-a-RELL-a.) The wonderful Roman word for the crispness of pizza tonda romana — onomatopoeic, mimicking the crunchy sound the cracker-thin crust makes when bitten.

The Neapolitan act of dividing bulk-fermented dough into individual portions and shaping them into balls. Performed by hand only, in continuous motion, traditionally using only a spatula.

The Italian flour grading system — describes fineness of grind and ash content, not protein. 00 is finest (talcum-fine), 2 is coarsest (whole-wheat-like). Says nothing about protein percentage; must be paired with a W rating to know if a flour is suitable for pizza.

The Italian measurement of flour strength, determined by an alvéograph test (blow air into a dough bubble until it bursts). Range: ~120 (very weak) to 400+ (very strong). Higher W = longer fermentation tolerance. American flour packaging does not show W ratings.

  • W 120–180: weak (cookies, pastries)
  • W 180–260: medium (focaccia, short ferment pizza)
  • W 260–320: strong (Neapolitan, 12–24h ferment)
  • W 320+: very strong (48h+ ferment, biga, poolish)

The standard check for gluten development. Pinch off a small piece of dough and gently stretch it between thumbs and forefingers. If it stretches into a thin, almost translucent membrane without tearing, gluten is fully developed. If it tears immediately, keep kneading 2–3 minutes and re-test.

(Plural: pizzaioli.) A pizza maker — specifically one who has mastered the craft, not just someone who reheats frozen pies. The art of the pizzaiolo napoletano is on UNESCO's intangible cultural heritage list.

The paddle used to launch pizza into and out of the oven. Wood for launching (less sticky), aluminum or stainless for retrieving (heat-tolerant). Pala also refers to a Roman pizza style baked on a long wooden peel, similar to al taglio but oval-shaped.

A Brief History of Pizza

From ancient flatbread to global obsession

Pizza is older than you think. Flatbreads topped with herbs and oil have been eaten in the Mediterranean for nearly 3,000 years — Etruscans, Greeks, and Romans all had versions. But the pizza we recognize today was born in Naples in the 18th century, when one specific ingredient changed everything: the tomato.

Timeline

~600 BC
Ancient Etruscans and Greeks bake flatbreads topped with herbs, oil, and cheese on hot stones.
~1500s
Round flatbreads called "pizza" — topped with herbs, oil, lard, or cheese — are popular Neapolitan street food. Tomatoes have just arrived from the Americas but are believed to be poisonous.
~1700s
Neapolitan poor begin topping flatbreads with tomatoes. This is the first true pizza in the modern sense — the marriage of wheat flatbread and tomato sauce.
1738
Antica Pizzeria Port'Alba opens in Naples — widely considered the world's first true pizzeria, originally selling pizza to street vendors and later to seated customers from 1830.
1866
Francesco DeBouchard's book "Customs and Traditions of Naples" describes pizza with cheese, basil, and tomato — the toppings that would later be called Margherita — already widely sold by street pizzaioli.
1889
The Margherita legend. Pizzaiolo Raffaele Esposito reportedly serves Queen Margherita of Savoy three pizzas at Naples' Capodimonte Palace. The pie with tomato, mozzarella, and basil — green, white, red of the Italian flag — wins her favor and bears her name forever. (Modern historians dispute the letter's authenticity, but the story endures.)
1905
Gennaro Lombardi opens Lombardi's in New York City, the first licensed pizzeria in America. Italian immigration brings pizza to the New World; it becomes a working-class staple in immigrant neighborhoods.
1933
First commercial pizza ovens manufactured in the US. Tavern-style thin crust takes hold in Chicago bars and Midwest taverns.
1943
Chicago deep-dish reportedly invented at Pizzeria Uno by Ike Sewell and Ric Riccardo. The thick, casserole-like pie becomes Chicago's tourist trademark.
1946
Detroit-style born at Buddy's Rendezvous on Six Mile Road. Gus Guerra and his wife Concetta develop a square pan pizza using surplus blue-steel automotive drip pans — birth of the frico edge.
1950s
Post-WWII Rome: bakers begin selling rectangular pan pizza by weight to fuel a fast-growing workforce. Pizza al taglio becomes street food.
1958
Pizza Hut founded in Wichita, Kansas. The chain era begins. Pizza spreads from immigrant enclaves to suburban America.
1984
AVPN founded in Naples by Antonio Pace and Lello Surace. The "Associazione Verace Pizza Napoletana" codifies rules for true Neapolitan pizza, defending it from global dilution.
2003
Gabriele Bonci opens Pizzarium in Rome. His high-hydration, long-fermented pizza al taglio creates the modern "Roman" style that pizza nerds worldwide now chase.
2010
European Union grants pizza Napoletana STG status (Traditional Specialty Guaranteed), protecting the method as cultural heritage.
2012
Andris Lagsdin launches the Baking Steel Kickstarter, inspired by Nathan Myhrvold's Modernist Cuisine. Home pizza-making is forever changed.
2017
UNESCO adds the art of the Neapolitan pizzaiolo to its Representative List of Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity.
2018
Ooni launches affordable home wood/gas pizza ovens. The 900°F crust at home becomes attainable for under $400.
Today
A golden age. Top pizzerias like Una Pizza Napoletana (NYC), Pepe in Grani (Caiazzo), I Masanielli (Caserta), Lucali (Brooklyn), and Bæst (Copenhagen) push the craft forward. Home pizza tools are better and cheaper than ever.

The Margherita Question

Every pizza book repeats the same story: in 1889, pizzaiolo Raffaele Esposito created the Margherita for Queen Margherita of Savoy, using mozzarella, basil, and tomato to mirror the Italian flag. It's a beautiful tale of food, monarchy, and nationalism.

The problem is that the story almost certainly isn't true. Historian Zachary Nowak has demonstrated that the famous "thank-you letter" from the royal palace has forged signatures, misplaced seals, and inconsistent handwriting. Pizzas with the same three ingredients were documented in Naples as early as 1866 — 23 years before the supposed royal visit.

What's likely true: pizza Margherita existed before 1889, was popular among Neapolitans, and was eventually associated with the queen's visit (possibly fabricated by the Brandi family decades later to boost their pizzeria's prestige). The myth has become as enduring as the pizza itself.

UNESCO and the Soul of Pizza

In 2017, UNESCO inscribed "the art of Neapolitan pizzaiuoli" on its list of intangible cultural heritage — putting it alongside flamenco, yoga, and Japanese sake-making. The recognition is not for pizza the food, but for the craft, the apprenticeship system, the gestures, and the community of pizzaioli who pass the knowledge generation to generation.

The submission noted that the pizzaiolo's craft includes "the kneading, the pirouetting motion of the dough, the management of the oven, and the sharing of the practice." The pizza you make in your kitchen is part of a 250-year-old tradition that traveled from a Neapolitan slum to every corner of the world.

Equipment

What you need · What to skip

Pizza tools have become an industry. Most of it is unnecessary. Here's an honest assessment of what makes a difference and what's marketing.

The Essentials

Digital kitchen scale

Non-negotiable. Volume measurements are too inconsistent. Look for ±1g precision, capacity to 5kg, and a tare button. The OXO Good Grips is a solid baseline at ~$50. For yeast under 5g, a separate jewelry-style scale with ±0.01g precision is worth $15.

Baking steel

The single biggest upgrade for home oven pizza. A 1/4" thick slab of steel preheated for 60 minutes at maximum temp conducts heat about 18× faster than a ceramic stone, mimicking what a 900°F deck does at 500°F. The original Baking Steel runs $119–149. Cheaper "pizza steels" on Amazon work too, but check thickness — anything under 1/4" doesn't hold enough heat.

Pizza peel

Wood for launching (less likely to stick), thin metal for retrieving (slides under the pizza easily). A 14" wooden peel and a 9" or 10" metal turning peel is the pro setup. Combined cost: $30–80.

Bench scraper

$8 of stainless steel. Used constantly: dividing dough, cleaning the bench, lifting stuck dough, scraping oil. You'll use this more than any other tool.

The Big Upgrades

Outdoor pizza oven (Ooni, Gozney, Solo Stove)

The category-killer for serious home pizza. These propane or wood-fired ovens hit 900°F+ — actual Neapolitan temperature. Pizza cooks in 60–90 seconds with real leopard char.

Pizza stone vs. baking steel

Different tools for different ovens. Stone belongs in a wood-fired oven, where ambient heat is already overwhelming and you want a more forgiving deck. Steel belongs in a home oven, where you need to compensate for lower air temperature with faster heat transfer. Using a steel in an outdoor pizza oven can actually burn the bottom — manufacturers warn against it.

Style-Specific

Detroit pan

Anodized blue steel. LloydPans (~$30) is the home reference. Detroit Style Pizza Co. makes a true reproduction of the original Buddy's pans. Cheap aluminum pans don't develop the same frico — anodized steel is non-negotiable.

Roman al taglio pan

Heavy aluminum rectangular pan (typically 12×16" or 16×24"). Generously oiled before the high-hydration dough goes in.

Cast-iron skillet

The poor pizzaiolo's deep-dish weapon. A 10" or 12" cast iron yields a Chicago-style or pan pizza with no special pan required. Preheat the skillet itself for crispier bottom.

Nice-to-Have

  • Dough containers ($30 for a stack) — Cambro or similar. Stack-friendly, see-through, made for cold ferment.
  • Infrared thermometer ($25–50) — Verify steel/stone deck temperature. Essential for outdoor pizza ovens, useful for home steel.
  • Pizza wheel — A sharp wheel cuts better than scissors. A rocking pizza knife (mezzaluna-style) handles thick crusts.
  • Squeeze bottles for olive oil — pro pizzerias use them for precision drizzling on the cornicione post-bake.
  • Dough whisk / Danish whisk — Easier than a wooden spoon for initial mixing of wet doughs.
  • Bench knife / dough scraper — In addition to the bench scraper. Slightly different tool for cleaner cuts.

Skip These

  • "Pizza stone with handles" — Handles defeat the purpose; the stone should be permanently in the oven preheating.
  • Rolling pins for pizza — Kills the crumb. Only acceptable for cracker-thin styles by design.
  • Branded "00 pizza flour" from non-Italian mills — Often re-bagged American flour with a marketing label. Stick to actual Italian mills (Caputo, Molino Grassi, Polselli) or use proper American bread/high-gluten flour.
  • Conveyor pizza ovens for home — Designed for chain pizzerias. Worse than a baking steel + home oven for most styles.
  • Pre-shredded mozzarella — Anti-caking starches interfere with melt and texture. Shred your own block.

Recommended Reading

  • Flour Water Salt Yeast — Ken Forkish. The home baker's bible. Beautiful, methodical, exactly what a beginner needs to advance.
  • The Pizza Bible — Tony Gemignani. Comprehensive: every style covered with multiple recipes.
  • Mastering Pizza — Marc Vetri. Modernist approach with deep technique.
  • Modernist Pizza — Nathan Myhrvold (3-volume set). The definitive scientific encyclopedia. $425 and worth it if pizza is your obsession.
  • Vito Iacopelli on YouTube — Free. Generations of Neapolitan technique demonstrated weekly.
  • Scott's Pizza Tours blog — Scott Wiener's column at Pizza Today. The historian of American pizza.
