Neapolitan

Naples, Italy · UNESCO heritage

The original. A soft, pliable disc with a puffy cornicione (raised edge) and a moist, almost wet center. Toppings are minimal — San Marzano tomatoes, fresh mozzarella, basil, a drizzle of olive oil. The dough bakes in 60–90 seconds in an 800–900°F wood-fired oven, which is what creates the signature leopard spotting — those black blistered char marks on the crust.

Hydration62%
Salt3%
OilNone
Flour00, 11–12% protein
Bake temp800–900°F ideal
Ferment8–24h room temp

Home oven workaround: Crank to 550°F with a preheated steel on the top rack for at least an hour, then run the broiler for 5 minutes before launching. Bake 2 minutes under broiler, then 2 minutes on bake. Bumping hydration to 65–68% helps the dough stay extensible through the longer bake.

New York

Italian-American · Foldable, by the slice

A thin, sturdy, foldable crust with crisp bottom and chewy interior. Built to support a generous load of low-moisture mozzarella and a sweet, slow-cooked tomato sauce. The slice is large enough to fold lengthwise — that's the test.

Hydration63%
Salt2.2%
Oil2%
FlourHigh-gluten, 13–14% protein
Bake temp500–600°F, 5–7 min
Ferment24–72h cold

Why oil matters here: At 500°F the dough has more time on the deck than Neapolitan. The 2% oil tenderizes the crumb so it doesn't go cardboard-stiff, and helps the bottom crisp without burning. Cold ferment for at least 24 hours; 48–72h produces the deep, slightly sweet, almost-sourdough flavor that defines a real NY slice.

Detroit

Square pan · Crispy frico edge

Born in Buddy's Restaurant in post-war Detroit, baked in blue-steel automotive drip pans. Thick, focaccia-like crust pressed into a well-oiled rectangular pan. The defining feature is the frico: cubed brick cheese pushed all the way to the pan walls, where it caramelizes against the metal into a crispy, lacy edge. Sauce goes on top of the cheese in racing stripes, applied after the bake.

Hydration70%
Salt2.5%
Oil3%
FlourBread, 12–13% protein
Bake temp500–550°F, 12–15 min
Ferment24–48h cold

The pan is the recipe: Use a 10×14" anodized steel Detroit pan, or a quality non-stick equivalent. Oil it generously — the dough should swim a little. Authentic versions use Wisconsin brick cheese; if you can't find it, a 50/50 blend of low-moisture mozzarella and mild white cheddar gets you close.

The Process

From mix to launch · The essentials

Mixing & Kneading

Combine dry, then add water

Whisk flour, salt, and instant yeast in a large bowl. Make a well, pour in your water at the target temp (see the Water Temperature tool). Mix with a spoon or hand until shaggy.

Autolyse (optional, recommended)

Cover and rest 20–30 minutes before kneading. The flour fully hydrates, enzymes start working, and you'll need to knead noticeably less. This is the cheapest dough upgrade there is.

Knead until smooth

Hand: 8–10 minutes. Stand mixer with dough hook: 5–7 minutes on low. The dough should be smooth, elastic, and tacky — not sticky. Stop when it passes the windowpane test: a small piece stretched between your fingers should form a thin translucent membrane without tearing.

Bulk ferment

30 min to 1 hour at room temp, covered. The dough should soften and start to puff. This step is when most of the structure forms.

Balling

Divide

Use a bench scraper and weigh each piece on a scale. Aim within ±5g — uneven balls cook unevenly.

Shape into taut balls

Pinch the underside while rotating, pulling skin down and tucking it under. Surface should be smooth and tight; seam should be on the bottom. Practice — your first ones will look wrong, and that's fine.

Rest seam-down

Place in a lightly oiled container, seam side down, with at least 2x the ball's volume in space for expansion. Cover airtight. Into the fridge for cold ferment, or onto the counter for room temp.

Stretching

Come to room temperature

Pull cold dough from the fridge at least 1–2 hours before stretching. Cold dough tightens up and tears. Warm dough — around 70–75°F — is supple and forgiving.

Flour the work surface generously

Don't be precious about flour at this stage. You can brush excess off the bottom before launching.

Press out from the center

Use fingertips, not palms. Press from the center toward the edge, leaving an inch of untouched rim — that's your cornicione. Push gas outward into the edge, don't deflate it.

Stretch over knuckles

Drape the disc over your knuckles (loose fists) and let gravity do most of the work, rotating slowly. For Neapolitan-style, never use a rolling pin — it kills the bubbles you spent days building.

Launching

Build on the peel, not the counter

A stretched dough left on the counter while you top it will stick. Move the stretched dough to a well-floured (or semolina-dusted) peel first, then top quickly.

Top sparingly

A thin layer of sauce, modest cheese, a few toppings. Too much weight = soggy, stuck dough that won't launch cleanly.

Shake test before launch

Give the peel a small back-and-forth shake. If the pizza moves freely, you're good. If it's stuck, lift the edge and toss flour underneath.

Launch with confidence

Tip the peel to about 20°, anchor the front edge on the stone or steel, and pull the peel out from under in one smooth motion. Hesitation is the enemy.

Troubleshooting

When something goes wrong · Tap to expand

Most likely Hydration too high for your flour. Strong flour (13%+ protein) handles 70%+ hydration; weaker flour starts struggling around 65%.

Also possible Under-kneaded — the gluten hasn't pulled the water in yet. Knead 3–5 more minutes and re-check.

Quick fix Generous flour on the work surface and your hands. Use a bench scraper instead of fingers to move it. Resist dumping flour into the dough itself — you'll change the whole recipe.

Next time Drop hydration 2–3% or switch to a stronger flour.

First check Tight and springy (snapping back) or soft and slack (spreading and tearing)? Tight = under-kneaded or too cold. Slack = overproofed.

Tight & tearing Cover it and rest 15–30 more minutes. Cold dough needs at least 1–2 hours at room temp before stretching.

Slack & tearing Overproofed — fermentation went too long. The gluten network has broken down. Reball gently if you can; bake sooner next time or use less yeast.

Always tearing Insufficient gluten development. Knead longer, use stronger flour, or try a 20-minute autolyse before kneading.

Most likely Yeast is dead or expired. Test it: 1/4 tsp yeast + 1/4 cup warm water (100°F) + a pinch of sugar. Should foam visibly in 5–10 minutes.

Also possible Water was too hot when mixing — anything over 130°F kills yeast. Or the room is too cold; below 60°F yeast activity drops significantly.

Less obvious Salt dumped directly on yeast can stall it. Always mix salt into flour first.

Patience play Cold-fermented dough barely rises in the fridge by design. The rise happens after you pull it out. Give it 2 hours at room temp.

Why it happens Too much time between stretching and launching. The dough's moisture seeps into the flour bed and pastes itself to the peel.

Prevention Build the pizza on the peel and launch within 60–90 seconds. Have all your toppings prepped before you stretch.

Better dust Semolina or rice flour rolls under the dough like ball bearings; regular flour absorbs moisture and glues things together.

Stuck right now Gently lift the stuck edge with a bench scraper and shoot more flour underneath. Shake the peel — if it moves freely, launch.

Most likely Oven not hot enough, or stone/steel not preheated long enough. Steels need a full 45–60 minutes to fully saturate.

Overproofed Long fermentation eats sugars — fewer sugars left = less Maillard browning. The crust stays pale even when fully baked through.

Quick fix Hit it with the broiler for 30–60 seconds at the end. Watch closely; pale to charred takes about 45 seconds.

Next time Preheat longer, bake higher, or shorten the ferment.

Most likely Underbaked. The bottom can look done while the middle is still wet. Stick a thermometer in the crust — done is 195–210°F internal.

Too many toppings A wet sauce or pile of cheese steams the dough from above. Less is more.

Cold dough launched too soon Cold dough from the fridge resists internal cooking. Always come to room temp first.

Underproofed Dough that didn't ferment long enough never develops the open structure needed to bake through.

Most likely Underproofed — gluten is still tight and tense.

Fix it now Cover and rest 30 minutes to an hour. The gluten will relax and stretching becomes easy.

Also possible You're trying to stretch cold dough straight from the fridge. Pull it 1–2 hours before you plan to bake.

Move the rack down Top rack gets direct radiant heat; middle rack lets the steel do more work.

Skip the broiler If you're using broiler-finish technique, you may be running it too long. 30–60 seconds is plenty.

Heat from below A baking steel (vs. a stone) conducts heat 18x faster from below — that's how you get bottom-crust char without top burn.

Glossary

The jargon, demystified · Tap to expand

A 20–60 minute rest after mixing flour and water, before adding salt and yeast (or before kneading). During this pause, flour fully hydrates, enzymes begin converting starch to sugar, and gluten starts forming on its own. Result: less kneading needed, more extensible dough, often better flavor.

The standard system for expressing dough recipes. Flour is always 100%. Every other ingredient is a percentage of flour weight. Water at 65% means 650g water per 1000g flour. The math makes scaling trivial and lets you compare any recipe to any other regardless of batch size.

An Italian-style pre-ferment: a stiff dough of flour, water (around 50% hydration), and a tiny amount of yeast, mixed 12–24 hours before the main dough. Adds complex flavor and improved structure. Compare to poolish, the wetter French version.

The first rise — when the dough ferments as one big mass, before being divided into balls. Most flavor and structure development happens here.

Letting dough rise slowly in the refrigerator, typically 24–72 hours. Cold slows yeast activity dramatically but doesn't stop enzymatic and bacterial activity that develops flavor. Cold-fermented dough is more flavorful, more extensible, and more digestible than same-day dough.

(Cor-ni-CHO-nay.) The raised, puffy edge of a Neapolitan pizza. The untouched border of dough that wasn't pressed flat during stretching, which puffs dramatically in a hot oven into an airy, blistered ring.

The temperature you want your dough to be at the end of mixing — typically 75–80°F for pizza. Hit it by adjusting your water temp using: water = (DDT × 3) − room temp − flour temp − friction. Friction is heat added by kneading: ~5°F for hand mixing, 15–25°F for a stand mixer.

The crispy, caramelized cheese edge that defines Detroit-style pizza. Cubed brick cheese is pushed all the way to the pan walls; in the hot oven, it melts, oozes against the metal, and crisps into a lacy golden crust around the perimeter.

The protein network formed when wheat flour meets water and gets agitated (kneading, mixing, folding). Made of two proteins — glutenin (strength) and gliadin (extensibility) — that bond into a stretchy web. Gluten traps the gas yeast produces, which is how dough rises and bread holds shape.

Water as a percentage of flour weight. 60% = 600g water per 1000g flour. Higher hydration = more open crumb, lighter texture, but stickier and harder to handle. Lower hydration = easier to work with but denser.

IDY (Instant Dry Yeast) goes straight into the flour; no proofing needed. ADY (Active Dry) needs to bloom in warm water first; use about 25% more than IDY. Fresh (cake) yeast is highly perishable; use about 3x the IDY weight. All do the same job — eat sugar, make CO₂ and flavor compounds.

The dark, almost-black blistered char marks on a properly baked Neapolitan crust. Caused by extremely high heat (800–900°F) puffing the dough's surface into bubbles that briefly touch the dome of a wood-fired oven. Marks of authenticity, not burning.

The browning chemistry between amino acids and reducing sugars that creates the golden color and toasty flavor in baked crust (and seared meat). Different from caramelization, which is sugar browning alone. Needs heat above ~285°F and isn't blocked by water, but moist surfaces brown more slowly.

Overproofed: dough has fermented too long. Gluten has broken down, structure is collapsing, dough is slack and tears easily. Result is pale, dense, deflated pizza.

Underproofed: dough hasn't fermented enough. Gluten is tight and tense, snaps back when stretched. Result is dense, gummy, bread-like crust without an open crumb.

A wet (100% hydration) pre-ferment originating in Polish baking. Equal weights of flour and water, a tiny amount of yeast, mixed 12–16 hours before the main dough. Adds flavor complexity and improved extensibility. Wetter and looser than a biga.

How to check gluten development. Pinch off a small piece of dough and gently stretch it between thumbs and forefingers. If it stretches into a thin, almost translucent membrane without tearing, gluten is fully developed. If it tears immediately, keep kneading.

The Italian flour grading scale (00, 0, 1, 2) refers to fineness of grind — 00 is the most finely milled, like silk. It's not a measure of protein. Protein in 00 flours varies: Caputo Pizzeria is ~12.5% (medium-strong), Caputo Saccorosso is 13%+ (strong). For 90-second wood-fired bakes, medium-strength 00 is ideal. For longer home-oven bakes, you may want stronger flour.
